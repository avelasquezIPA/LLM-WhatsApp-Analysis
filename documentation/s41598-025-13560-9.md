# A comprehensive qualitative analysis of patient dialogue summarization using large language models applied to noisy, informal, non-English real-world data

### Anderson A. Ferreira 1,3  , Leonardo Rocha 2 , Washington Cunha 1 , Ana Cláudia Machado 2 , João Marcos Campos 1 , Gabriel Jallais 1 , Adriana C. F. Viana 4 , Elisa Tuler 2 , Iago Araújo 1 , Víctor Macul 5,6 , Olívio Souza Neto 5 , Antônio Pereira de Souza Júnior 5 , Giordano de Pinho Souza 5 , Joice Marques Pallone 5 , Mariana Aparecida Dumbá Soares 5

### Welton Augusto Santos 5  & Marcos André Gonçalves 1

This study evaluates the ability of Large Language Models (LLMs) to summarize real-world dialogues
between patients and the healthcare team of an e-health company that provides digital healthcare
services, primarily communicating via WhatsApp. The team needs quick access to patient information
to deliver accurate and personalized responses. Summarizing past messages is the approach examined
here, aiming for concise, non-redundant, and truthful summaries that capture the main dialogue
characteristics despite facing (real-world) noisy and informal content in an under-represented
language - Portuguese. To do so, we collected an anonymized Portuguese dataset of WhatsApp
messages exchanged between patients and the healthcare team. Dialogue quality was assessed for
size, readability, and correctness before generating summaries with LLaMA3 and Qwen2 using specific
prompts. Volunteers evaluated these summaries on coverage, relevance, redundancy, and veracity
using a 5-point Likert scale. Our qualitative and quantitative experimental results indicate that LLMs
can produce effective summaries of dialogues between patients and healthcare teams, even when
faced with low-quality data in an underrepresented language. This is a surprising result due to the
challenging scenario. Among the tested LLMs, LLaMA3 demonstrated a slight edge over QWen2 in
coverage and veracity among the evaluated methods. Our results demonstrate a potential to build
real-world practical services to assist healthcare professionals in responding to patient messages
with agility, clarity, and cohesion, enhancing both communication efficiency and patient satisfaction.
Ultimately, the advocated approach could significantly improve the landscape of online healthcare
communication, particularly in resource-constrained settings like Brazil, where access to primary care
is limited.
The increasing use of electronic health records as well as of (instant) messaging platforms such as WhatsApp
and Messenger has expanded the use of e-health portals to exchange messages between patients and healthcare
teams 1 . The management of such an increasing volume of messages is a challenge. Indeed, primary care
physicians are estimated to spend around 1.5 hours daily analyzing approximately 150 patients’ messages 1 . To
respond effectively, health professionals must understand the message’s context by reviewing previous ones from
the same patient, as well as any other relevant patient information available to them.

In Brazil, where 34% of the population lacks access to primary care, it is estimated that achieving 100%
coverage would require 236,900 healthcare professionals at a cost of BRL 22.9 billion/year 2 . An approach to
scaling such services would be through a Digital Primary Care model, such as that offered by Ana Health
( https://www.anahealth.com.br /  ), an e-health company that provides digital healthcare services, primarily
communicating via WhatsApp, in order to scale patients. Ana Health’s healthcare professionals primarily
interact with the population through a specific messaging system – WhatsApp. In this interaction model, the
challenge of processing and comprehending a large volume of messages remains, as it is essential to respond to
each patient individually.
In more detail, at Ana Health, when a healthcare team member receives a new message from a patient, to
adequately respond, the team member shall comprehend the full context, review previous messages exchanged
with the patient, and understand the current message. Only after that can the healthcare team member craft
a response aimed at maintaining patient engagement. For the patient to receive the response efficiently and
effectively, it is essential to quickly gather contextual information in order to provide accurate, up-to-date, and
personalized responses.
An approach to assist the healthcare team in understanding the context of patient messages, which we
advocate and analyze in this article, is to apply summarization techniques to prior messages exchanged between
the patient and the healthcare team. Such a summarization, if successful, can also support other services the
healthcare team offers to the patient, such as psychotherapy, chronic disease monitoring, and drug prescription.
Previous work has developed and evaluated summarization techniques 3 – 5 , including the summarization of
clinical data 4 , exploiting techniques ranging from rule-based approaches to the use of Large Language Models
(LLMs) 6 . However, these techniques are rarely evaluated in real environments using real data 7 . Our work tries
to fulfill this literature gap. That is, our objective is to evaluate the ability of LLMs to generate useful summaries
for assisting a healthcare team in understanding a patient context using real and anonymized health data and to
conduct a thorough evaluation of these summaries with the support of specialists, particularly volunteers from
a healthcare team.
Recent advances in LLMs based on transformers have helped in several tasks related to natural language
processing, including summarization 6 . In 8 , the authors state that LLMs can assist in several healthcare tasks, such
as pre-consultation, diagnosis, and management, with proper development and supervision. Despite that, some
concerns and challenges still remain in the use of LLMs in healthcare, including 8 – 10 : (1) data privacy - ensuring
that real patient data will not be leaked and used by the owners of the LLMs for other purposes; (2) credibility
and correctness of information - LLMs can perpetuate incorrect information or produce “hallucination”; and (3)
biased data - LLMs are commonly trained with diverse data that may be biased and, consequently, may generate
biased responses.
Back to the task on focus, in this article, we leverage summarization – a process of reducing a long text into a
shorter version that preserves important information for understanding the original text 5 , 11 . We aim to evaluate
the LLM’s ability to summarize WhatsApp (full) dialogues between patients and a healthcare team. Ideally, such
summaries should capture the main characteristics of the dialogues, be concise, non-redundant, and contain
truthful information.
Specifically, we propose evaluating open-source and publicly available LLMs in the task of summarizing
patient messages, aiding healthcare professionals with patient-oriented, concise, quicker-to-read information to
assist in responding. Moreover, such a summarization should enable healthcare teams to scale personalized care
management by tailoring approaches to diverse patient needs, serving as a foundational step toward developing
a digital primary care AI agent. To evaluate the feasibility of such an approach, we utilize data from real
environments, including real (anonymized) interactions between patients and healthcare teams, and conduct an
evaluation of these summaries with the support of volunteers from a real healthcare team.
A few studies have been conducted on summarizing clinical data. Particularly, in 4 , the authors reviewed
publications on data summarization in the clinical context. The summarized data include mainly patient’s
electronic health records. The authors also discuss the importance of such summaries for helping tasks related to
intensive care units, surgery, diagnostics, hospital care, chronic disease monitoring, oncology, drug prescription,
psychotherapy, etc. Our work differs from 4  in two main aspects: (1) data source – we explore data coming from an
instant messaging system, particularly input messages from dialogues between patients and the healthcare team;
such messages are usually noisy as we shall demonstrate in our analyses, potentially impacting summarization
quality; and (2) application domain – our main purpose is to assist a healthcare team in responding to new
patient messages, ensuring personalized, humanistic, and contextually-appropriate responses.
In sum, the main and novel contribution of this work is the evaluation of the ability of two large language
models (LLMs), LLaMA 3 and Qwen 2, to summarize real-world WhatsApp dialogues between patients and
healthcare teams based on real-world, noisy, and informal non-English (Portuguese) data. The study highlights
the potential of LLMs to improve digital healthcare communication, particularly in resource-constrained
settings like Brazil, where access to primary care is limited.

### Analyzing message quality

Before summarizing the dialogues, we analyze the message’s textual quality to understand data limits and the
potential impact of data quality on the final results. Low-quality texts can lead LLMs to generate incoherent or
inaccurate summaries or summaries that do not contain the text’s main information. In addition, spelling or
grammatical errors may be interpreted by LLMs as unknown or inappropriate words or concepts. We based our
analysis on the methodology proposed by Hasan et al 12 . We analyzed three textual quality dimensions: (1) size,
(2) readability, and (3) correctness.
Size
Total number of characters, words and sentences. According to Hasan et al. 12 , messages of higher quality are
usually neither too short nor too long. We also define long messages as those exceeding the average length by
at least ten words, and short messages as those containing five words fewer than the average message length or
less, similarly to 12 .

### Summarizing the dialogues

As mentioned, our main goal in this work is to evaluate the LLMs capability in summarizing (patient, healthcare
team) dialogues. Ideally, such summaries should capture the main dialogue aspects and include no wrongful
information. For the sake of reproducibility and transparency, we focus our study on publicly available opensource LLMs that can be run locally, ensuring data privacy, as we do not send any private patient data to remote
closed LLMs such as GPT. In our experiments, we evaluated Qwen 2 (Qwen2-7B-Instruct) and LLaMA 3 (MetaLlama-3-8B-Instruct) as LLMs to summarize dialogues. We selected LLaMA 3 and Qwen 2 based on several key
considerations:

1. Open-Source Availability and Data Privacy: Both models are open-source, allowing for local deployment

without reliance on external cloud infrastructure. This aspect is crucial for our application, as it enables the
system to operate without transmitting sensitive user data over the internet, thereby mitigating potential
risks related to data privacy and security, especially relevant when considering future production-level adop­
tion by the partner company.
2.    Empirical Performance: LLaMA 3 and Qwen 2 have demonstrated state-of-the-art performance across a
range of natural language processing tasks, including question answering, classification, code generation,
text rewriting, and reasoning. Public benchmarks and evaluations (e.g., Meta AI blog on LLaMA 3 and Hug­

For the summarization process, all messages from a (patient and healthcare team) dialogue are grouped together
to generate a single document (possibly a long one). More formally, for each patient  p i  belonging to a set of
patients  { p 1 , p 2 ,  · · ·  p n } , we produce a document  d i  with all messages related to  p i . A summarization method
receives a document  d i  for  p i  as input and must produce a summary  s i  capturing the main aspects and topics
discussed in  d i .
For using LLMs as a summarization method, we developed a prompt, illustrated in Fig.  1 . This prompt
contains instructions for performing the summarization task. The text to be processed by a LLM must be
tokenized, i.e., it is broken down into smaller subword units known as tokens, resulting in a sequence of tokens.
Each subword is identified numerically. The LLM processes the sequence of tokens and generates a different
sequence to create the respective summaries. Figure  2  depicts the full summarization process.
LLMs can only receive a limited number of tokens as input. In our work, as we deal with messages in
chronological order, we use the last 5,000 tokens from a dialogue as input to the LLMs, as the last messages of
the dialogue may be more important to respond to a current patient message.

### Evaluating the summaries

To evaluate the LLM-generated summaries, we performed an A/B test. We conducted the evaluation of the
generated summaries using volunteers from Ana Health’s healthcare team. It is important to stress that the
participants who evaluated the summaries were not involved in the dialogues with the patients to avoid any bias
that may come from previous knowledge of the patient.
For evaluation, we did not exploit traditional summarization metrics, such as ROUGE 16 , BLEU 17 , NIST 18 ,
and METEOR 19 , which are based on text overlap, or BERTScore 20  and BARTScore 21 , which are similarity-based
metrics, as these metrics require ground-truth information, which is not available in our dataset. In other words,
we do not know what would be the “perfect summary”. Some recent works also analyze the use of LLMs as
summary evaluators 22 – 24 . However, our goal is not to analyze the ability of LLMs to evaluate the quality of
summaries. Instead, we focus on evaluating their performance in generating useful summaries about patients,
when facing noisy and informal data, with the goal of helping a healthcare professional to appropriately respond
to the patient’s messages. Thus, we conducted an evaluation by humans, in particular potential real users of the
generated summaries. This constitutes one main contribution of our work.

### Evaluation criteria for the summaries

We elaborated a survey in which each participant had to read messages from the original entire dialogue
(conversation) and, next, rate each summary using a 5-point Likert scale (1 - strongly disagree, 5 - strongly
agree) 22  considering four perspectives:
•    Coverage: The summary covers all important and relevant aspects of the full conversation;
•    Relevance: All aspects of the summary are relevant to the context of the messages, i.e., the summary does not
contain irrelevant portions;
•    Redundancy: The summary is non-redundant, i.e., it does not contain repetitive information;
•    Veracity: The summary is truthful (does not contain wrongful information).
After reviewing the literature 3 , 25  and discussing the metrics with the Ana Health healthcare team, we focus our
assessment on these perspectives. A supplementary file contains an example of our survey.
Participants
As volunteers/participants to evaluate the summaries, we had five physicians and 19 psychologists. We selected
24 healthcare professionals who are responsible for welcoming patients, conducting triage based on patientreported information, and maintaining an accurate understanding of each patient’s history and most recent
interactions with the company. It is also important to highlight that the literature indicates that usability testing
with more than 15 users is typically sufficient to identify the vast majority – if not all – of the major issues 26 .
We distributed the survey among participants to ensure a balanced representation of professional types. As
additional information, the participants are predominantly women, with approximately 22% being men. Each
summary was evaluated by two women and one man, or by three women.
Evaluation protocol
In the survey, we applied the same evaluation protocol to all participants (both external and internal) to
minimize bias in our results. We estimated that, to maintain an evaluation time of around 15 minutes, each
participant should evaluate two summaries to avoid fatigue and ensure effective participation throughout the
study, including the external psychologist. Several studies 27 , 28  in the area of research methodologies indicate that
surveys lasting less than 15 minutes tend to have a higher response rate, fewer dropouts midway through the
questionnaire and better quality responses. For each summary, participants first read the full dialogue, then read
the summary, and finally rate the four perspectives based on the Likert scale.
Before randomly selecting the dialogues to be summarized in our experiment, we first filtered dialogues with
a token count between 1,000 and 5,000. This step ensures that the dialogues will be neither truncated by the LLM
nor too brief to contain insufficient information for generating a summary. Next, we randomly selected eight
full dialogues and provided them to both LLMs — LLaMA 3 and Qwen 2. Consequently, we obtained sixteen
summaries for evaluation, with each summary assessed by three different participants.
Analyzing survey data
To analyze the survey data, we first analyze the distributions of responses in each perspective, aiming to obtain
an initial understanding of the behavior of the participants’ evaluations for each LLM. Then we use the numerical
scale (1 - strongly disagree – 5 - strongly agree) to analyze the mean values and their standard deviations to
obtain a quantitative interpretation of the LLMs effectiveness.

### Data privacy

This study was conducted in accordance with the highest ethical standards and received prior approval from an
independent institutional ethics committee. Specific attention was given to the issues of data anonymization,
privacy preservation, and the ethical implications of processing patient-related communication.
The dataset used in our study was anonymized prior to our access, with all identifiable information removed
by the partnering company in compliance with their internal data governance policies. We did not perform any
data collection ourselves. Instead, we received the pre-processed data in anonymized form, with no access to any
personal identifiers such as names, contact details, or healthcare identification numbers.
Furthermore, all data processing, including the summarization task, was conducted locally using on-premise
infrastructure. At no stage were the data or derived outputs transmitted to cloud-based services or external
servers. This approach ensures that the risk of data exposure through insecure communication channels is
eliminated.
To strengthen our commitment to confidentiality, all members of the research team signed a legally binding
Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA) with the data-providing entity, ensuring that no part of the data or results
could be used or disclosed outside the scope of the approved study.
Regarding the operational deployment of the system, it is important to clarify that only authorized healthcare
professionals who are already granted access to the original patient communications within the company’s clinical
workflow will be able to access the summaries generated by the system. These professionals are, by definition,
bound by professional confidentiality and ethical obligations, and already possess access to more detailed and
potentially identifiable information than what is present in the summaries. Therefore, the summarization
component does not introduce any additional privacy risks, nor does it expand the circle of data access beyond
what is already sanctioned by the healthcare provider.
Our study applied state-of-the-art anonymization techniques, removed direct identifiers, and ensured that
all work was conducted under strict legal and ethical safeguards. These measures significantly mitigate the risk
of deanonymization and are consistent with ethical research practices and data protection regulations such as
national data protection laws applicable to our context.

### Results

### Evaluating the quality of the messages

In this section, we discuss the quality of the messages regarding three dimensions: size, readability, and
correctness.
Size
Figure  3  shows the distribution of the size of messages in words, characters, and phrases, considering all messages
exchanged by patients and the healthcare team, whereas Table  1  shows average, maximum and minimum values
of characters, words, and phrases - along with their quartile boundaries - considering all messages. Up to 75% of
the messages have up to 135 characters, 25 words, and 4 phrases, respectively.
We found 47,443 (23.45%) long messages and 112,971 (55.84%) short messages. That is, most of the messages
are short, while almost a quarter are long.
Readability
We utilized the Flesch-Kincaid grade level for the readability analysis. Before applying the Flesch-Kincaid grade
level, we grouped all messages to and from the same patient, resulting in three types of “documents”: (i) all
messages sent by the healthcare team to a patient  p i  and all the messages sent by  p i  to the healthcare team, and
(iii) the union of both, i.e., all messages from or to each patient (all messages per patient). As discussed above,
we separated the analysis according to the direction of the message flow. Figure  4  and Table  2  show the results.
Comparing the grade levels of the groups of messages sent by the healthcare team with those of the groups of
messages sent by patients, we observed higher average values and quartile limits for those sent by the healthcare
team. This indicates that, on average, texts produced by the healthcare team are more complex and less simplistic
than those produced by patients (they require a higher educational level to be read). The difference is noteworthy
and should be considered in the development of future methodologies that rely on this type of data. The
distribution of grade levels for the messages sent by patients has a wider range of values, meaning that patient
messages have a higher variability in terms of quality.
Correctness
For this analysis, we used a Portuguese dictionary from  br.ispell  to compare the proportion of correctly spelled
words in messages sent by patients with those sent by the healthcare team. The results are shown in Fig.  5  and
Table  3 .
Notice that healthcare team messages exhibit a higher level of correctness compared with those sent by
patients. Patients’ messages have very low correctness, indicating poor writing quality, containing a lot of typos,
misspellings, abbreviations, unknown words, etc.

Kappa-Cohen (agreement between participants)
Since the Kappa-Cohen coefficient is only meaningful when calculated in evaluations carried out on the same
object (summary), we selected four summaries generated by LLaMA 3, represented in Table  6  as  s 1 ,  s 2 ,  s 3  and
s 4 , and four summaries generated by Qwen 2, represented as  s 5 ,  s 6 ,  s 7  and  s 8 , totaling eight summaries. Since
three participants evaluated each summary, we have three Kappa-Cohen values for each summary and show the
average value of these three values in Table  6 .
Kappa-Cohen values range from -1 to 1, with values lower than 0 indicating that the agreement is worse than
what would be expected by chance. We observed such a situation in the evaluations of summaries  s 5  and  s 7
generated by Qwen 2. Values between 0.0 and 0.2 indicate weak agreement, which we observe in various results.
Values between 0.2 and 0.4 indicate reasonable agreement, as occurs in  s 3  and  s 8  generated by LLaMA 3 and
Qwen 2, respectively.
Overall, when analyzing the values obtained by applying the Kappa-Cohen coefficient, we note that the
agreement values were generally low, with LLaMA 3 results being more consistent than Qwen 2’s. However, it is
important to stress that since this metric considers only total agreement (same response), it is very sensitive to
the fact that there are five possible responses. In other words, there is no consideration of the  degree of gradation
of the responses, being the disagreement between “agree” and “strongly agree” considered the same as between
“strongly agree” and “strongly disagree”, for example.

### Discussion

Previous work on summarizing clinical data has not delved deeply into the quality of the input data, which, in
our case, as we shall see, can be very poor and informal. This analysis itself is a contribution of this work, as it
puts a high burden on the LLM-based summarizers. On top of that, we have the fact that the dialogues are in
Portuguese. Indeed, while being one of the most spoken languages globally, Portuguese has historically been
underrepresented in training many large language models (LLMs) compared to languages like English, Spanish,
or Chinese. This can be attributed to several factors, including the availability of high-quality training data, the
focus of research and development efforts, and the predominant use of English in the tech industry 30 .

### Message quality

Regarding the size dimension, we observed that most messages were considered short (56.12%) and around
23.58% were considered long. Short texts may lack information or contain incomplete information, while long
texts may have redundancies, which may lead LLMs to not focus on the main parts of the text. Both issues may
affect summary quality.
The average Flesch-Kincaid grade levels for the healthcare team and patients suggest that a higher grade
level may be required to understand healthcare team messages compared with those from patients. Regarding
patients, we observe that their messages have a wider range of values for the Flesch-Kincaid grade level than
those from the healthcare team. Although this may indicate a potential diversity of patients in terms of education
level, several other factors may also influence these results, such as the level of detail needed to talk about certain
subjects and the use of audio to explain more complex subjects, among others.
Regarding correctness, most words in the messages sent by the healthcare team are found in a standard
dictionary, while this trend is not found for patient messages. Indeed, the proportion of “correct” words in
patient messages is very low, around 44% on average. However, this low correctness level may be taken in context
and be more carefully analyzed as the language usage of WhatsApp users has its own distinct terms, such as
“vc” (abbreviation of “você” – you in Portuguese), “tbm” (abbreviation of “também” – also in Portuguese), “pq”
(abbreviation of “porque” – why or because in Portuguese), in which, from the perspective of the users are
common and seen as correct. In any case, the high occurrence of incorrect or non-standard words may lead
LLMs to ignore or misinterpret them in the summaries, causing omissions of important parts from the original
text or generation of wrong information.
Overall, considering the dimensions evaluated of size, readability, and correctness, there are clues of
low quality in patients’ messages. This (poor) data quality in our dataset may pose significant challenges for
summarization tasks using LLMs.

### Summarization quality

We evaluated the summaries from four perspectives, coverage, relevance, redundancy and veracity, through
an online survey. Both LLMs performed well from all four perspectives. More than 50% of the responses are
“strongly agree” and “agree” in all perspectives and in both LLMs. This shows the ability of the LLMs to generate
satisfactory summaries despite the quality of the original text. The few “strongly disagree” responses obtained
in our analysis (less than 10%) are concentrated on Qwen 2 summaries, being completely absent in LLaMA 3’s
coverage.
Comparing the results of LLaMA 3 with those of Qwen 2, considering “strongly agree” and “agree” responses,
we notice that, for coverage and veracity, LLaMA 3’s summaries are slightly superior to those produced by
Qwen 2. Both LLMs tie under the relevance perspective and the Qwen 2 summaries are slightly superior regarding
(lack) of redundancy. Overall, considering all evaluation criteria, LLaMA 3’s summarization performance in our
application is slightly superior to Qwen 2’s.
In an external qualitative analysis, viewed from the perspective of a newly integrated professional into the
healthcare team, the professional observed that the summaries produced by LLaMA 3 are objective, free of
irrelevant repetitions and do not contain false or dubious content. On the other hand, the summaries produced
by Qwen 2 do not meet the same standard. While a summary correctly reflects the content of the dialogue, the
other one lacks important data included in the dialogue.
Positive evaluations from the analyzed perspectives potentially mean summaries that can effectively help
team members quickly understand the context of a new message without needing to review the entire prior
dialogue with the patient. This also enables team members to respond to the new message in an efficient and
contextually appropriate manner. Such agility in providing clear and accurate information may help reduce the
anxiety of patients awaiting a response, thus making the interaction process between patients and healthcare
team members more efficient.
In short, despite the messages being written in Portuguese, some clues of limited quality of the messages
and high variability in the grade level required to understand the messages, the summaries produced by both
evaluated LLMs are satisfactory for the sake of the target task, with LLaMA 3 showing slight superiority.

Our work has some limitations. First, we generate summaries using messages exclusively exchanged between
patients and the Ana Health healthcare team. Our goal in this work is only to evaluate the ability of LLMs to

generate summaries. If we aim to create summaries containing complete patient information, such as their health
history, preferences, and demographic details, additional data sources will be required. Second, our evaluation
of the summaries was carried out with members of the Ana Health healthcare team. Perhaps people from other
areas or even patients may have different opinions. Third, we evaluated the summaries using only the Likert scale.
If we wanted to understand in depth the reason for each value recorded by the participant, we would need other
types of responses, such as comments on the evaluated summaries. Fourth, our evaluation used messages from
the dialogues of eight patients as a source. A more comprehensive analysis of LLMs summarization capability
may require a larger number of dialogues. Fifth, this work did not evaluate the use of summaries in other tasks,
such as their impact on responding to patient messages.

### Future directions

We are currently exploring several avenues to enhance the performance and applicability of the summarization
system in future work. These efforts include incorporating electronic medical records to enrich the input data,
integrating audio processing to capture and utilize verbal communication, and adopting strategies that enable
the separate analysis and summarization of distinct segments of the data prior to generating a comprehensive
final summary.
Furthermore, in our qualitative analysis of LLMs’ ability to summarize dialogues, we deliberately limited the
amount of input data to ensure it remained within the context window of the models. At this stage, we consider
this a reasonable constraint, as our primary goal was to assess how effectively the models could capture the
essence of the most recent interactions between patients and the healthcare team – under the assumption that
recent messages are more likely to reflect the patient’s current health status. As part of our future work, we intend
to investigate methods to address the limitations imposed by the context window size of large language models,
which remains a critical factor influencing summarization quality 5 , 31 .
In this work, an external psychologist evaluated two summaries according to the same protocol applied to
all participants. In the future, we also intend to include more external evaluators for improving our evaluation.

### Conclusions

In this article, we evaluated the ability of two large language models, LLaMA 3 and Qwen 2, to summarize
dialogues between patients and healthcare teams. These dialogues, written in Portuguese, were conducted
through WhatsApp. Ideally, the generated summaries should highlight important information about each
patient, enabling the healthcare team to respond to new patient messages quickly and in a personalized manner.
Differently from previous work, which applied summarization in high-quality clinical data (e.g., electronic
health records), we start our contributions by first assessing the quality of the dialogues to be summarized
and found evidence of low data quality, especially due to informality. The primary objective of our study is to
rigorously evaluate whether state-of-the-art LLMs – specifically open-source models – are capable of producing
clinically useful summaries from real-world, informal, and noisy patient-provider dialogues in Portuguese, a
language underrepresented in current medical NLP research.
Our contribution lies in the application and systematic evaluation of these models in a realistic healthcare
communication scenario, which presents several technical and practical challenges: the dialogues are
unstructured, colloquial, and often fragmented, reflecting actual communication patterns in digital health
platforms; the setting involves asynchronous, chronologically evolving dialogues, requiring the models to
maintain contextual coherence over multiple messages; the domain involves sensitive health-related content,
where summarization errors can have non-trivial implications.
To address concerns regarding the robustness and safety of the generated summaries, we structured our
evaluation around four key criteria: coverage, relevance, redundancy, and veracity. These dimensions were chosen
precisely to assess potential summarization errors that could impact clinical interpretation. All evaluations were
conducted manually by human annotators familiar with the healthcare domain and native in Portuguese. Our
results suggest that, while both models perform reasonably well, there are notable differences in their ability to
generate true and relevant summaries – issues that are critical in any health-related application.
To summarize, the novelty of our work lies in: (1) the application and comparative evaluation of LLMs in
a low-resource, high-stakes domain using real-world Portuguese clinical dialogues; (2) the design of a multifaceted evaluation framework specifically tailored to identify errors that could compromise clinical decisionmaking; and (3) the establishment of a foundation for future clinical validation, which will directly address
utility and patient safety concerns raised by the reviewer.
In conclusion, even when processing low quality Portuguese texts, the evaluated LLMs were able to produce
summaries that could significantly improve patient care. These summaries support healthcare team members
in understanding the context of new messages from patients, enabling them to respond quickly and in a
personalized manner, thereby enhancing patient outcomes.
From a broader perspective, our study underscores the crucial role of LLMs in addressing healthcare
disparities. In countries like Brazil, where a significant portion of the population lacks access to basic healthcare
and there is a shortage of health professionals, digital healthcare services are not just essential, but they represent
a critical solution. Strategies, such as those discussed in this article, that can effectively process large volumes of
data in underrepresented languages have the potential to make a significant social impact by helping to alleviate
pressing issues.
